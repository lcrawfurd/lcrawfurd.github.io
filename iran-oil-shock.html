Step 1: Oil prices → Food prices

ΔPfood = ((Poilnew − Poilbaseline) / Poilbaseline) × ε

Where ε is the pass-through elasticity — how much a 1% oil price rise increases food prices. We decompose into two channels:

  • Transport: oil → diesel → transport costs → food retail price
  • Fertilizer: oil → natural gas → ammonia/urea → crop production costs (natural gas is 70–90% of ammonia production cost)

Note on sources: The total pass-through of ~0.22 is well-supported in the literature (Baffes 2007: 0.18; World Bank 2016: 0.19; Baffes & Dennis 2013: 0.25; Dillon & Barrett 2016: 0.26 for East Africa). However, the split between transport (0.10) and fertilizer (0.12) channels is our assumption — it is not directly estimated in any single paper. The sensitivity tab shows how much this matters.

Step 2: Food prices → Real income loss

Real income lossq = sq × ΔPfood

Where sq is the food expenditure share for quintile q. The poor spend more of their income on food, so the same food price increase hits them proportionally harder.

Note: Q1 food share of 65% is consistent with World Bank SSA household surveys (60–69% for the bottom quintile). Q5 of 20% is low for pure low-income countries (where even the richest quintile spends ~40% on food) but reflects the full developing world including middle-income urban populations. Adjust in the sidebar to explore.

Step 3: Count who falls below the poverty line

Nnew poor = ∑i 1[yi(1 − sq(i) · ΔPfood) < z] − ∑i 1[yi < z]

We generate 200,000 synthetic individuals from a log-normal income distribution calibrated to the target mean income and Gini, reduce each person's effective purchasing power, and count how many cross below the food insecurity threshold ($2.50/day).

Policy interventions

PolicyHow it works in the model
Cash transferAdd transfer amount to effective income of bottom X%
Food subsidyGovernment absorbs X% of food price increase for everyone
Price controls70% price reduction, but 20% face shortages (modeled as 30% welfare loss)
Key caveats: This is a stylized model, not a forecast. Log-normal distribution is an approximation. Pass-through varies enormously by country (0.03–0.40+). The model is static — no supply response, exchange rate effects, or coping strategies. Pass-through takes 6–12 months to materialize.

Policy comparison

Why coverage matters more than generosity: The people newly pushed into food insecurity are those just above the poverty line — roughly the 20th–35th percentile. A smaller transfer to the bottom 30% reaches these marginal people. A larger transfer to only the bottom 20% mostly goes to people who were already food insecure before the shock. In a crisis, reaching the right people matters more than the size of the transfer.
Cash transfers work best when:
  • Markets are well-integrated, supply can respond
  • The shock is a price crisis (food exists but costs more)
  • Urban areas with good transport
Food subsidies / in-kind work best when:
  • Markets are thin/remote (Kandpal et al. 2023)
  • The shock is a supply crisis (less food produced)
  • Distribution infrastructure exists (e.g. India's PDS)
Cash transfers in thin markets (Kandpal et al. 2023, REStat): In the Philippines, a targeted cash transfer raised perishable food prices in remote markets and increased stunting among non-beneficiary children by 11 percentage points. An oil→fertilizer shock reduces food supply — pumping cash into supply-constrained markets can make prices worse. Best approach: cash in urban areas, direct food provision in remote areas.
How much of a cash transfer goes to food? (Bellemare, Kandpal & Thomas 2026): Using 5 RCTs across 4 countries, BKT estimate the income elasticity of food expenditure at just 0.03 for routine transfers — meaning only 3% of extra income goes to food. In a crisis the share is likely higher (households are cutting meals, not optimising). We default to 50% but this is an assumption. Use the “Share of transfer spent on food” slider to explore. At 0.03, cash transfers are almost ineffective at reducing food insecurity; at 1.0, they work dollar-for-dollar.
Price controls don't work: High prices reflect real scarcity. Countries that tried to freeze food/fuel prices saw shortages, queues, smuggling, and black markets. In the model, price controls are worse than doing nothing.

Core simulation function

function computeImpacts(oilPriceNew, policy, transferUsd, transferCov) {
  // Step 1: Oil -> food prices
  const oilPctChange = (oilPriceNew - oilBaseline) / oilBaseline;
  const foodPriceChange = oilPctChange * totalPassthrough;

  // Step 2: Real income loss (varies by quintile)
  const realIncomeLoss = foodShare[i] * foodPriceChange;
  const effectiveIncome = incomes[i] * (1 - realIncomeLoss);

  // Policy: cash transfers add to effective income
  if (policy === 'cash_transfer') {
    if (incomes[i] <= percentile(incomes, transferCov))
      effectiveIncome += transferUsd;
  }

  // Step 3: Count who crosses below poverty line
  newPoor = count(effectiveIncome < povertyLine)
          - count(incomes < povertyLine);
  return newPoor * population;
}

Income distribution

// Log-normal calibrated to mean income and Gini
// Gini = 2*Phi(sigma/sqrt(2)) - 1  ->  solve for sigma
// mu = ln(meanIncome) - sigma^2/2
incomes = lognormal(mu, sigma, N=200000);
